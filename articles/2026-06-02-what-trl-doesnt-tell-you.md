# What TRL Doesn’t Tell You

*First published in Future-Ready Ventures on LinkedIn, 2 June 2026.*

A few months ago, I wrote a short post about Technology Readiness Levels.

It began with a simple observation: TRL was never meant to be a full map for venture building.

The post opened up a much bigger conversation than I expected. That probably says something about how many people have seen the same problem from different angles.

TRL is useful. I do not think that is the issue. It gives scientists, engineers, funders and institutions a shared language for describing whether a technology is still an early concept, a laboratory result, a prototype, a demonstrated system, or something already proven in the field.

In technical environments, that common language matters.

The problem begins when we ask TRL to do more than it was designed to do.

A technology can move up the readiness scale and still not be ready to become a venture.

That is the gap I keep coming back to.

Invention and venture are not the same thing. A patent is not a business. A prototype is not a market. A technical demonstration is not adoption. A research breakthrough, however important, does not automatically become something customers understand, trust, buy and use.

There is a lot of work between those two worlds.

That work is often invisible.

And because it is invisible, it is often underfunded, underestimated, or left too late.

The comfort of a ladder
I understand why people like TRL.

A ladder is comforting. It gives a sense of order. You start at one level and move to the next. You can point to progress. You can report progress. You can show a funder, board, university or partner that the technology is moving forward.

But ventures rarely move like that.

A team may spend months improving the technology, only to discover that the original customer segment is wrong. A prototype may perform well in controlled conditions, but not fit the messy environment where it must eventually operate. A market may show interest, but not urgency. A potential partner may like the idea, but not enough to change procurement, workflow, budget or risk appetite.

From the technical team’s perspective, progress is happening.

From the market’s perspective, nothing may have changed.

That is where many IP-rich ventures get stuck. They become more technically mature, but not necessarily more investable, adoptable or scalable.

This is not a criticism of scientists, engineers or inventors. In many cases, they are doing exactly what they were trained to do: improve the technology.

The missing work is different.

It is the work of translation.

Translation is where value starts to become visible
The most difficult stage in commercialising IP is often not the invention itself. It is translating the invention into a form that other people can act on.

Researchers need to explain why the science matters beyond novelty.

Founders need to explain who has the problem, why it matters now, and why existing solutions are not enough.

Customers need to see how the technology fits into their world, not just how it performs in a demonstration.

Investors need to understand how uncertainty will be reduced over time.

Partners need to see where they fit, what they contribute, and what they gain.

This is why the journey from IP to venture is not only a technical journey. It is also a communication journey, a trust-building journey, a market-learning journey and a capital-design journey.

A technology may be impressive. But if people cannot understand how it becomes useful, valuable and deployable, it remains trapped inside its own promise.

That is the real valley of death.

It is not only a funding gap.

It is a translation gap.

The market does not begin with the technology
Many IP ventures begin with what has been invented.

That is natural. The invention is the starting point. It may have taken years of research, experiments, failures, publications, grant applications, patents and prototypes to reach that point.

But the market does not begin there.

The market begins with a problem.

A cost someone wants to reduce. A risk someone wants to avoid. A process someone wants to improve. A regulation someone needs to comply with. A performance gap someone can no longer ignore. A pressure that has become painful enough for people to act.

If the technology does not connect with that reality, maturity alone will not carry it forward.

This is one of the hardest shifts for IP-led teams. The question is no longer only, “Can we make this work?” It becomes, “Where does this matter enough for someone else to change?”

Change is expensive. Even when the technology is good, customers still need to learn it, trust it, integrate it, pay for it and justify it internally.

That is why vague market interest can be misleading.

Many people will say an idea is interesting. Fewer will offer time. Fewer will provide data. Fewer will run a serious pilot. Fewer still will pay, adopt, recommend or build around it.

Venture readiness starts to become real when interest turns into commitment.

Timing can change everything
One of the most overlooked parts of readiness is timing.

Sometimes the market is already waiting. The problem is obvious, alternatives are poor, and customers are actively searching for something better. In those cases, a technology can move quickly once it proves enough.

In other sectors, regulation creates the opening. Climate, sustainability, health, infrastructure, safety and finance often work this way. A new rule, disclosure requirement, approval pathway or compliance pressure can turn an idea from “interesting” into “necessary.”

There are also cases where the technology is simply too early.

The science may be strong, but the surrounding system is not ready. There may be no procurement route, no budget owner, no implementation partner, no data infrastructure, no regulatory clarity, or no customer who feels the pain strongly enough.

This is why timing should be treated as part of venture readiness.

A venture does not become ready just because the technology improves. It becomes ready when enough surrounding conditions begin to align: market need, customer trust, regulation, infrastructure, capital, partnerships and execution capacity.

When those conditions come together, progress can accelerate.

When they do not, even excellent technology can sit on the shelf for years.

Investors are reading a different map
Founders can feel frustrated when investors hesitate.

From the founder’s perspective, the technology may be real. The prototype may work. The IP may be protected. The team may have already overcome difficult scientific or engineering challenges.

So why is the investor still unsure?

Often, it is because the investor is not only asking whether the technology works. They are trying to understand what kind of uncertainty remains.

Some uncertainty is technical. Some is commercial. Some is regulatory. Some is operational. Some is about the team. Some is about timing. Some is about whether the capital required to reach the next milestone makes sense.

A higher TRL may reduce technical uncertainty, but it may leave many other questions untouched.

This is why a technically mature project can still feel too early for commercial capital. It is also why a technically less mature idea can sometimes attract serious interest if the problem is urgent, the customer is clear, the team is credible and the pathway is well structured.

The investor is not always looking for certainty. Early-stage ventures rarely offer that.

What investors need is a believable way to reduce uncertainty.

That is a very different conversation from simply reporting technical progress.

Capital is part of the design
Another mistake is treating capital as something that comes after the venture has been designed.

For IP-rich ventures, capital strategy is part of the venture design itself.

Not every venture fits the same funding path. Some need grants and non-dilutive capital before they are ready for private investment. Some need corporate partners because adoption depends on industry access. Some need patient capital because the development cycle is long. Some need licensing revenue before they can support a broader company. Some need government, infrastructure or procurement alignment before the market becomes real.

The wrong capital can distort a venture.

It can push the team to promise speed when the technology needs patience. It can force a product into a market before the system is ready. It can make founders chase growth metrics that do not fit the nature of the technology.

The right capital does something different. It gives the venture the right kind of time.

That does not mean unlimited time. It means time matched to the real development pathway.

For deep tech and IP-led ventures, this matters enormously.

Capital is not just fuel.

It shapes the route.

The customer is the strongest reality check
Internal readiness assessments have their place, but they can become too comfortable.

Teams naturally want to believe they have moved further than they have. After years of effort, it is human to feel that the next level has been reached.

But the real world has a way of testing that belief quickly.

A customer does not care what level a team thinks it has reached.

The customer cares whether the solution works in their environment, with their people, under their constraints, within their budget, and against their risks.

This is why early customer engagement is not something to postpone until the technology is “finished.”

In many cases, the customer helps reveal what finished should mean.

A serious customer conversation can reshape the use case. A pilot can expose hidden integration issues. A procurement discussion can reveal whether the buyer is really the user. A failed sale can teach more than another internal review.

This kind of learning can be uncomfortable, but it is essential.

The market is not just a destination.

It is part of the development process.

One technology may contain many readiness levels
Even technical readiness is more complex than one number.

As technologies move closer to deployment, they often become systems of systems. The core science may be strong, while packaging, manufacturing, software, maintenance, data, compliance, training, deployment or integration remain much less mature.

This is especially true in deep tech, climate tech, medtech, industrial technologies and complex digital systems.

A venture may not have one TRL. It may have a cluster of readiness levels across different components.

One weak component can hold back the whole system.

That is why integration matters so much.

A technology does not scale because one part works beautifully in isolation. It scales when the parts can work together in the world.

And the world is rarely clean.

It has legacy systems, budgets, politics, habits, approvals, procurement cycles, risk committees, internal champions, sceptics, delays and competing priorities.

A venture has to be ready for that world too.

Not all IP wants to become a startup
There is also a point we should say more openly.

Not every piece of IP should become a venture-backed company.

Some IP is better licensed. Some belongs inside an existing company. Some should become a joint venture. Some should become a product line, service model, platform, standard, tool or partnership.

Some should remain in research until the timing improves.

This is not a failure of ambition.

It is a more honest way to create value.

The startup form is powerful, but it is not always the right form. Forcing every promising invention into a startup can waste years of human effort and misdirect capital. It can push researchers into roles they do not want and create companies that exist mainly because no one paused to ask whether another pathway would work better.

Good venture building is not about turning everything into a startup.

It is about finding the right structure for the IP to create value.

Sometimes that structure is a company.

Sometimes it is not.

The work between
I still believe TRL has a useful role.

But it should be treated as one lens, not the whole map.

The deeper work begins when we ask what else needs to become ready.

Maybe the customer needs to be clearer. Maybe the regulatory path needs to be understood. Maybe the business model needs to be rebuilt. Maybe the manufacturing assumptions need to be tested. Maybe the system fit is weak. Maybe the capital strategy is wrong. Maybe the team needs a different kind of support. Maybe the technology is strong, but the story is not yet believable to the people who need to act on it.

These are not always tidy questions.

They require judgement, conversation and translation between people who often speak very different languages.

But that is the real work between IP and venture.

The future will not be shaped only by the best inventions. It will be shaped by the people who can turn inventions into forms the world can understand, trust, adopt and scale.

So the next time we ask, “What is the TRL?”

We should probably pause and ask something more useful:

“What work is still missing between this technology and a real venture?”
